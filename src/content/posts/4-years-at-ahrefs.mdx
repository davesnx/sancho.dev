# The tech

Our secret? Everything is written in OCaml. The backend, frontend, crawler, devops, internal tools, and even the CI.

The short answer for why OCaml (and as brief as I can be, since I‚Äôm not entitled enough) was that we needed an expressive and performant language on the crawler and it went extremely well, so well, that it spread across the entire backend and eventually the frontend.

Originally, we had PHP as our backend, but after using OCaml for our crawler, it quickly took over the entire backend stack. On the frontend, we were using React, and at that point we had to choose between TypeScript (or Flow, remember that?) and Reason. Since Reason is essentially OCaml with JavaScript-friendly syntax sharing the same type system, semantics, tooling, and ecosystem.. adopting it was a no-brainer.

This gave us something powerful: a truly unified tech stack.

Our REST endpoints share the exact same type definitions as our frontend. Our SSR server reuses the same React components as the client. Our data validation logic runs identically on both sides. This creates a single source of truth: change a type definition once, and the compiler catches mismatches across the entire stack.

But this isn't just about type-safety or sharing code. We use the same package manager (opam), the same build system (dune), the same LSP for our editor, and deploy everything through a single pipeline. One language to learn, one mental model to master. It lowers the learning and the context switching. A newcomer joins the team, learns a single way of thinking, and suddenly they can contribute anywhere.

While Reason is the language of choice for the frontend, we use Melange to compile to JavaScript. We're actually one of Melange's main sponsors, and I'm part of the core team - but more on that later.

Aside from the unusual language of choice, there‚Äôs another particularity that didn‚Äôt surprise me when I met the team. The engineering team is pretty much NIH maxxing.

 In a small ecosystem like OCaml, building for the long term means owning your dependencies. We use some third-party tools like ClickHouse and buildkite, but most pieces are built in-house. It sounds extreme, but I'd rather modify and maintain code I understand than trust something I can't control.

# Learnings

Finally, the learnings. Might be highly personal and contextual but still somehow applicable in other places.

## Bootstrap companies rock

The CEO makes decisions based on his view of the company, the market. Appears in technical discussions, shares his quiet view. Pushes products and pushes the team forward without external pressure. Previously I have been in companies where entire roadmaps gets shaped by investors opinions, which conflicted with CEO's vision of companies.

## Remote but not strangers

There's a clear personal barrier with remote working. The person who has been reviewing your code for years, it's only an avatar and luckly they have their face.

This could be isolating and counter-productive, but ahrefs does something clever here. Being profitable (very profitable, actually) and tech-focus means we can invest in sponsoring events such as (ICFP)[link], (fun-ocaml)[link] or (React Alicante)[link] and bringing the team together. Also, we do one ski retreat per year and other hacking-together events.

These gatherings transform our remote culture. Back home, when someone challenges your PR, you hear their actual voice in your head, not some imagined critical tone. You know they're coming from a place of wanting the best solution because you've seen them fight just as hard for their own ideas at 3 AM in some conference hotel lobby.

## The culture can be daunting

The culture is a mix of international people but with eastern european roots, inherit from our Ukrainian founders. Direct communication, pragmatism, rationality over anything else.

Being async-first shapes everything. Discussions happen in public threads that might run for weeks. No quick Slack DMs or hallway conversations - everything is written, searchable, and thoughtful. This forces precise communication and builds trust across time zones.

Coming from Spain, this caught me completely off guard. I'd worked at two US-culture companies before, where feedback came wrapped in compliment sandwiches and party emojis. The Spanish way? We soften criticism with warmth. At Ahrefs? Pure, unfiltered directness.

Here's what I mean: If your solution has problems, someone will simply write "This won't scale. Try approach B instead." No emoji cushioning, no "great effort but...", no paragraph explaining how valued you are before the critique. In most places, even terrible solutions get celebrated with "Good start! üéâ" followed by gentle suggestions.

At first, this felt harsh. But after a few months, I could confidently say it's incredibly effective. The solution isn't attached to your identity neither personality, it's just code or decisions that can be improved. Once you internalize this, you iterate faster with actually valuable feedback instead of decoding what people really think.

This efficiency extends everywhere. With less than 1 hour of meetings per week, I can actually focus on solving hard problems instead of talking about solving them.

## Being technology first

Our CEO codes, our CTO codes. By focusing in OCaml, we are able to hire talented people.

By bringing passionate people together it generates great solutions and great energy. server-reason-react was born out of passion and to face a big challenge, that eventually payed off.
